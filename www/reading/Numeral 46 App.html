Numeral 46    Wu Ji

Hexagram: Mountain on Earth

Heavenly Revolutionary Star

Hour Pillar Ji Chou – Earth Ox Ji Mao – Wood Rabbit
Metaphor Drinking alone at the moon pavilion. Boating with the current.
Explanation Placed in a prosperous situation. Decisions must be firm. No obstructions in whatever undertakings.

Heavenly Annoying Star

Hour Pillar Ji Si – Earth Snake Ji Wei – Earth Goat
Metaphor Butterfly in the spring garden. Firefly emerging from decaying grass.
Explanation Born into a rich and noble family, no worries about enjoying an affluent lifestyle. A lot of clever plots on your mind that can turn failure into winning situations.

 Heavenly Eyebrow Star

Hour Pillar Ji You – Earth Rooster Ji Hai – Earth Pig
Metaphor Seeing the moon emerging from the clouds. Moon over the sea shining in autumn waters.
Explanation Misfortune is history, something good is at hand. You have an open mind and there is no worry.

 

Destiny:
It is not just a lonesome life, but also one where your relatives feel you bring bad luck to them, because the timing of your birth coincided with something untoward happening. It is important for you to find the positive features from any shortcomings.  If you are a man, when it comes to marriage, instead of your wife moving in with your family, you move to become part of your wife’s family1.  To avoid bad luck, you should hand over your son to be adopted by your brother.  Your destiny is such that you are not meant to become the guardian of the family business. In the early days you experience difficulties, yet you can get rich late. Before you are really old, the grey hairs come out on your head. There is much ado about nothing.  In this life, you want to do so much that it feels like there are thousands of things which cannot be accomplished.  There are three things that are left open-ended in your life: the debts you bring to your parents, the karmic debts from when you cursed the Buddha in a previous life, the debts from raising your children. When you see Buddha you are angry and do not worship Buddha. If you want to be successful in the end, you must accept failure in the beginning. From the root of the Chinese goldthread2 plant, grows the licorice root. Who believes sweetness comes from bitterness.  


Luck Verses

Foundation: 
Great ambition, follow the General to fight enemy,
Bad timing, not met good luck, unfortunately.
In Pig or Goat year, a different hat to wear,
As you climb the ladder of success to heaven.

Life Path:
Dragon and Horse years, time to spread your wings.
Enter phoenix city, at Rat and Ox timings,
Where you’ll reveal the success in your life,
You’ll have days of support for a future that’s bright.

Marriage:
Dancing and singing in the jade ballroom.
Unnoticed,  the mandarin ducks fly after the rain.
A lot of wild birds in your humble living room,
Keep you company amongst the green pine and bamboo.

Children:
One fruit, when flower withers in late spring.
Expect one fruit to be most extraordinary.
His bright future aided by your hidden virtue.
In your late years, he brings glory to the family.

 

Harvest:
What is happening after your passing?
In Snake or Dog year, it is time to go.
Your companions are cranes and baboon,
Enjoy the cool breeze and the bright moon.

 

Style: Jade Bangle Breaks
What a pity when the jade bangle is broken.
No matter how skillful you are, it cannot be mended.
Then go overseas to look for five feet tall corals.
Mandarin ducks are being frightened at dawn by the moon.
Walking alone with bamboo sticks, in shoes made from straw.
On top of white cloud mountain, a medicine box made from a dried calabash is given to the fairy Lu3.

 

Footnote 1
In traditional Chinese culture, when a couple marry, the woman goes to live with the husband’s family.  If a man moves in with the wife’s family, he becomes part of that family and any children will take on the wife’s surname.  A wealthy family with only daughters (and no sons) would often seek a son-in-law, usually from a less affluent family, to marry and move in with them.

Footnote 2 
Chinese goldthread (Coptis chinensis), a rhizome used in medicine as a herb to get rid of toxic substances.

Footnote 3
Lu Dongban, one of the eight immortals.

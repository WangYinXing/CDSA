Numeral 5    Jia Wu

Hexagram: Thunder on Mountain

 



Heavenly Gloomy Star

Hour Pillar

Wu Zi – Earth Rat

Wu Yin – Earth Tiger  
Metaphor Horse pulling a cartload of salt. Dry well is receiving rain.
Explanation Cherish your skills and talents, even when you cannot put them into practice. When you are totally exhausted, there is someone coming to help.

Heavenly Flooding Star 

Hour Pillar Wu Chen – Earth Dragon Wu Wu – Earth Horse
Metaphor Dressed beautifully walking in the dark. Planting a pine tree on the rocks.
Explanation Know that you have built a good foundation, even if the timing does not seem right. If there is only a shallow foundation, do not expect things to last.

Heavenly Noble Star

Hour Pillar Wu Shen – Earth Monkey Wu Xu – Earth Dog
Metaphor Plum blossoms open in 10th lunar month. The road leading to the flat plain.
Explanation You become prosperous in your early years. You do not like to flatter others. Life is just smooth for your entire life.


Destiny:
This life is that of a lazy star and a gloomy star.  Whatever you may do, you regret your actions.  Whether walking or sitting down you cannot make up your mind. You do not like to owe anyone a favor.  You do not know how to embellish your actions with charm. Whilst there is no need to worry about making a living, you need to change. If you hold onto your inheritance, you will lose support from relatives and friends. If you sit down for too long you will have illness of your four limbs. Leave your hometown to make a fortune1. What should be done quickly you take it slowly. When you should be taking your time, you act hastily.  Your character is quite contrary in what should be quick and slow. There is no clear success or failure. Whenever you face danger or obstacles, there will always be someone coming to the rescue.


Luck Verses

Foundation:
When there are happy times, enjoy, feel pleased.
Why lower your ambitions? There is no need.
During Tiger years move forward, get ahead.
Like the powerful tiger prowling in the forest.

Life Path:
Leave your hometown and you will succeed.
Live a strict and honest life, you will feel much peace.
Like a general enjoying victory, on the border of the territory.
When your spirits are low, make changes and grow.

Marriage:
No need to worry about marital harmony.
When your boat first sets sail, strong winds may blow.
How will the relationship be? You want to know.
Imagine at twilight a pair of ducks swimming together.

Children:
With fewer sons and more daughters.
They will marry into wealthy families.
Enjoying being rich and famous,
They live a fortunate life, most leisurely.

Harvest:
Flowers bloom into full beauty, vying for attention.
After wind and rain goes on day and night,
Flowers are all gone, not a pretty sight.
In Ding and Ren years forget big dreams, lose ambition.

 

Style: Fisherman Bringing in the Nets
When the catch is brought in, the boat is light.
The soldiers raise the standard to march out of the castle.
The general on the border misses the home country.
On the road away from home, both lows and highs,
Make good plans to leave whilst you are a youth.
Soar upwards like a goose through open skies.
When the flowers are gone and the moon is full,You’ll have many good memories, that’ll be the truth.

 

Footnote 1 
The original text talks about how a son would be handed over for adoption by a paternal uncle or a man would marry into a family and his children would take the mother’s surname.  If a wealthy family only had daughters, then in order to continue the family line, one of the son-in-laws would allow his children to take his wife’s surname. In both cases the man would be leaving his hometown, therefore, in a modern context, it has been translated as ‘leave your hometown’
